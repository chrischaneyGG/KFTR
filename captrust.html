The Big Story

Samoa's Lone Rep in the 2026 IPP Class

When Kaylan Faumui boarded his flight for Fort Myers in January, he became the only athlete from Samoa selected for the NFL's 2026 International Player Pathway class — one of just 15 picked globally.

Ten weeks of training. One pro day. A shot at an NFL roster.

Faumui's path is the version of the pipeline most fans never see: not the five-star recruit signing on Twitter, but the on-island talent identified by an international scout, lifted by his family, and put through an accelerated NFL development track.

"This is exactly why the work matters. One spot, on one list, can change a family's trajectory for three generations."

The IPP class of 2026 is the smallest class Samoa has had in three years — and that's the story behind the story. The pipeline is real, but it's also fragile when access stays bottlenecked.

Three Things to Watch
1 Which NFC West and AFC West teams take the closest look — the West Coast Polynesian pipeline runs deep there.
2 Whether his pro day numbers move him from practice-squad track to active-roster track.
3 What it signals for the 2027 class — KFTR is already tracking four on-island prospects who could make next year's list.

The First NIL Check:
Three Mistakes Athletes Make

1
Treating it like income, not revenue.
It isn't a paycheck. There are quarterly taxes, agent fees, and business expenses sitting inside that number. A good rule: assume 30–40% of any NIL deal is already spoken for before it hits your account.
2
Skipping the LLC conversation.
Once NIL money becomes consistent, an LLC isn't optional — it's how you protect your family, your future earnings, and your eligibility paperwork.
3
Not telling your parents.
Especially in Pasifika families, money is family. Hide it and you create resentment. Share it, plan it together, and you build something multigenerational.

Aiga Spotlight

A Host Family in Provo

Three years. Six athletes hosted. Two now in the NFL pipeline.

"We don't host athletes. We host sons. The football part is just what they happen to be good at."
— Mama Tuilagi
What's the hardest part of hosting?
"Sending them back. Every single time."
What surprises new host families?
"How much they teach you. We thought we were the ones giving."
Why do it?
"Because someone did it for my husband in 1994. We're just paying the same bill forward."
